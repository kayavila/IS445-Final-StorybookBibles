This project is a collaboration between Kay Avila, an UIUC iSchool IS 445 student, and Dr. Melody Green, a professor at the Urbana Theological Seminary. Dr. Green is researching children’s storybook bibles.

Children’s Storybook Bibles

Storybook bibles are illustrated retellings of stories from the Christian Bible, intended for children as their audience.

Dataset Creation

Dr. Green’s faculty photo on the UTS website.

Dr. Green went through her collection of physical books and provided the ISBNs for all of the books From there, Kay wrote a

few python scripts
These scripts resolved the book data using the OpenLibrary and ISBN DB service APIs. The scripts themselves are available on GitHub at https://github.com/kayavila/isbn-indexer.
to download information about the books and create a dataset about Dr. Green’s collection. She also used OpenRefine to clean up the data and attempt to make it more consistent.

The information about each book includes:

  • ISBN
  • Title
  • First author and any other authors
  • First illustrators and any other illustrators
  • Publisher
  • Publisher location
  • Year published
  • Number of pages
  • MSRP

However, not all of the data is complete, as not all information was available for all books. Dr. Green ultimately hopes to use some of the statistical information about this dataset within her publications, though it will require additional manual labor to double-check the information from the ISBN databases against the information in the books themselves.

Exploration

Number of Books Published by Year

Most of the books in the collection were published between 1980 and 2015, with the number gradually increasing by year published.

Number of Pages Versus Price

MSRP stands for “manufacturer suggested retail price,” or the price that the publisher suggests the book should be sold at.

Within the collection, the number of pages seems roughly correlated with the suggested price. However, a lot of the data for the number of pages seems missing.

Top Ten Values

There are also a number of interesting “top ten” values, such as the top ten most profilic authors or illustrators in the collection, and the top ten most common publication locations.

These were generated with pandas, matplotlib, and Jupyter notebook.

Wordcloud

Finally, it can be interesting to look at a body of text and visualize the relative frequences of words appearing using a word cloud.

We can see that the word “Bible” appears the most often, closely followed by “First”. Other common words include “Pretty”, “Candle”, and “Stories”.

This was generated using pandas, matplotlib, Jupyter notebook, and Andreas Mueller’s WordCloud python library.
